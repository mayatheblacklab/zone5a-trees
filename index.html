Zone 5a Front Yard Trees: Purple & White Flowering, 10 ft Max

Six recommended small trees and large shrubs for maximum seasonal bloom

USDA Zone 5a (-20°F to -15°F) Purple and/or white flowers 10 ft maximum height Maximum bloom output Full sun preferred

Bloomerang Purple Lilac Reblooms

Syringa × 'Penda' (Proven Winners trademark)

Images show Syringa vulgaris (common lilac) as a representative. Bloomerang Purple is a compact trademarked cultivar with identical deep-purple flower structure.

Highlights

  • Only reliably reblooming lilac on the market
  • Blooms spring, then again late summer through frost
  • 4–6 ft tall × 4–6 ft wide; naturally rounded
  • Deep purple, highly fragrant flowers
  • Hardy to Zone 3; thrives in 5a
  • Deer resistant

Pros

  • Two full flush bloom periods per year — unmatched
  • Compact scale suits narrow or small front yards
  • Strong fragrance fills surrounding area
  • No major pruning needed to maintain size
  • Extremely cold-hardy; very reliable in 5a
  • Pairs perfectly with a white companion plant

Cons

  • Requires deadheading after spring flush for best rebloom
  • Fall rebloom is lighter than the spring flush
  • Takes 2–3 years to establish strong bloom pattern
  • Needs full sun (6+ hrs); poor performance in shade
  • Susceptible to powdery mildew in humid summers (cosmetic)

Miss Kim Lilac

Syringa pubescens subsp. patula 'Miss Kim'

Highlights

  • 4–9 ft tall; naturally upright oval shape
  • Icy lavender-purple flowers, intensely fragrant
  • Blooms 2 weeks AFTER common lilacs — extends season
  • Burgundy-purple fall foliage color
  • Hardy to Zone 3; extremely reliable
  • Among the most fragrant of all small lilacs

Pros

  • Later bloom extends purple flowering into early June
  • Exceptional disease resistance (better than common lilac)
  • Beautiful 3-season interest: flowers, foliage, fall color
  • Tolerates a range of soil types
  • Very long-lived once established
  • Good as specimen or informal hedge

Cons

  • Single bloom period only (no rebloom)
  • Can approach 9 ft without light pruning
  • Takes 3–5 years to bloom profusely after planting
  • Growth rate is moderate to slow
  • Lavender-pink, not a deep purple tone

Dwarf Korean Lilac 'Palibin'

Syringa meyeri 'Palibin'

Highlights

  • 4–5 ft tall × 5–7 ft wide; perfect dense mound
  • Smothered in lavender-pink flowers each spring
  • Hardy to Zone 3 — among the hardiest ornamentals
  • One of the most popular compact ornamental shrubs sold
  • Naturally stays compact with no pruning needed
  • Fragrant; slightly lighter scent than common lilac

Pros

  • Virtually no maintenance once established
  • Perfect scale for most front yards — stays tidy
  • Excellent disease resistance; rarely troubled by pests
  • Very heavy annual bloom — one of the showiest small shrubs
  • Works as specimen, mass planting, or low hedge
  • Extremely widely available at garden centers

Cons

  • Single bloom period only
  • Lavender-pink rather than deep purple
  • Slower growth than common lilac
  • Needs full sun for best bloom density
  • Very common — less unique as a landscape specimen

Josee Lilac Reblooms

Syringa × 'Josee' (S. meyeri hybrid)

Images show Syringa patula, a closely related parent species. Josee (S. meyeri hybrid) has nearly identical compact lavender-pink flowers with a similar fragrance profile.

Highlights

  • 4–6 ft tall; dense, rounded compact habit
  • Lavender-pink fragrant flowers in spring + again late summer
  • One of the original reblooming lilacs
  • Hardy to Zone 3; robust in Zone 5a
  • More compact growth than Bloomerang at maturity
  • Good disease resistance throughout season

Pros

  • Reblooms spring and late summer — extended color
  • Very compact; works in tight front yard spaces
  • Fragrant throughout both bloom flushes
  • Slightly smaller than Bloomerang at maturity
  • Works well massed in groups of 3 for a hedge effect
  • Good cold hardiness; reliable in harsh winters

Cons

  • Rebloom is sporadic and lighter than spring flush
  • Lavender-pink, not deep purple
  • Very similar to Bloomerang — redundant if both are planted
  • Needs full sun for reliable rebloom
  • Less widely stocked than Bloomerang or Palibin

White Fringe Tree Native

Chionanthus virginicus

Highlights

  • 8–20 ft naturally; can be pruned to stay under 10 ft
  • Spectacular drooping 4–6 in. white fringe flower clusters
  • Blooms late spring (May–June) — after lilacs
  • Eastern North American native; Zone 3–9
  • Male plants showier; female plants produce blue-black fruit
  • Excellent pollinator plant; supports native bees

Pros

  • Uniquely spectacular white flowers unlike any other small tree
  • Native plant — far superior wildlife value vs. ornamentals
  • Extremely adaptable: tolerates clay, wet soil, part shade
  • Outstanding pest and disease resistance
  • Attractive blue-black fruit and yellow fall foliage
  • Extends the white bloom window after lilacs finish

Cons

  • Requires annual pruning to stay reliably under 10 ft
  • Slow growing — patience needed to reach flowering size
  • Blooms late (May–June); no spring-early bloom
  • Leaves emerge very late in spring, looking dead until May
  • Less widely available than lilac options; may need specialty nursery

Dwarf White Lilac

Syringa vulgaris 'Dwarf White' (and similar white cvs.)

Images show Syringa vulgaris (common lilac) for structural and scale reference. White-flowered cultivars ('Dwarf White', 'Mme Lemoine', 'Vestale') have identical structure with pure white blooms and the same classic fragrance.

Highlights

  • 5–8 ft tall; compact form of common lilac
  • Pure white flowers with classic, intense lilac fragrance
  • Spring bloom (April–May) aligned with Bloomerang's first flush
  • Hardy to Zone 3; extremely cold tolerant
  • Excellent white contrast to purple companion plants
  • Notable cultivars: 'Dwarf White', 'Mme Lemoine', 'Vestale'

Pros

  • Pure white blooms create strong color contrast in purple/white scheme
  • Classic lilac fragrance — strongest of all options listed
  • Once established, nearly maintenance-free
  • Excellent cut flower; brings fragrance indoors
  • Very cold-hardy; performs reliably every year in Zone 5a
  • Widely available and affordable at most garden centers

Cons

  • Single bloom period only
  • Can exceed 8 ft without pruning; needs occasional shaping
  • Susceptible to powdery mildew in humid conditions (cosmetic)
  • Tends to sucker at the base if not monitored
  • Needs full sun and good air circulation
  • Foliage is unremarkable after bloom period ends
